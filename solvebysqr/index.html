Solving by Taking the Square Root

Step-by-step Guide:

1. Isolate the term containing the square of the variable (e.g. \(x^2\)).

2. Take the square root of both sides of the equation.

3. Remember to consider both the positive and negative roots of the square.

Example 1: \(x^2 = 16\)

Following the steps above, we first isolate \(x^2\) - it's already isolated in the equation.

Then we take the square root of both sides: \(\sqrt{x^2} = \sqrt{16}\).

This gives us \(x = \pm4\) - both +4 and -4 are solutions since they square to be 16.

Example 2: \(2m^2 = 18\)

We first isolate \(m^2\) by dividing both sides of the equation by 2: \(m^2 = 9\).

Then we take the square root of both sides: \(\sqrt{m^2} = \sqrt{9}\).

This gives us \(m = \pm3\) - both +3 and -3 are solutions since they square to be 9.
