Alpha & Beta Decay - Chemical Equations

Alpha Decay:

In alpha decay, a radioactive element emits an alpha particle: a Helium-4 nucleus (containing 2 protons and 2 neutrons). The atomic number (Z) decreases by 2 and the mass number (A) decreases by 4.

The general equation is \( ^{A}_{Z}X \rightarrow ^{A-4}_{Z-2}Y + ^{4}_{2}He \).

Example 1: \( ^{238}_{92}U \rightarrow ^{234}_{90}Th + ^{4}_{2}He \)

Example 2: \( ^{210}_{84}Po \rightarrow ^{206}_{82}Pb + ^{4}_{2}He \)

Example 3: \( ^{226}_{88}Ra \rightarrow ^{222}_{86}Rn + ^{4}_{2}He \)

Beta Decay:

In beta decay, a neutron inside the nucleus of a radioactive element changes into a proton and an electron (beta particle). The electron is emitted and atomic number (Z) is increased by 1 as number of protons increase with the neutron changing into a proton.

The general equation is \( ^{A}_{Z}X \rightarrow ^{A}_{Z+1}Y + ^{0}_{-1}e \).

Example 1: \( ^{14}_{6}C \rightarrow ^{14}_{7}N + ^{0}_{-1}e \)

Example 2: \( ^{90}_{38}Sr \rightarrow ^{90}_{39}Y + ^{0}_{-1}e \)

Example 3: \( ^{40}_{19}K \rightarrow ^{40}_{20}Ca + ^{0}_{-1}e \)
